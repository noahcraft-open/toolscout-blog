## What Is a QR Code?

A QR (Quick Response) code is a 2D barcode that encodes information — typically a URL, but also text, contact info, WiFi credentials, or payment data. Modern smartphones can read them instantly with the built-in camera app.

### QR Code Anatomy

A QR code has several components:
- **Finder patterns**: The three square corners that help scanners locate the code
- **Alignment patterns**: Help correct for distortion in larger codes
- **Data modules**: The black and white squares that encode your content
- **Error correction**: QR codes can be read even when 30% damaged (Level H)

## Choosing the Right QR Code Type

### URL / Website QR Code
The most common type. Encodes a web address. When scanned, opens in the phone's browser.

**Best practices:**
- Use a short URL or URL shortener to keep the QR code less dense
- Test on multiple devices before printing
- Add a call-to-action near the QR code ("Scan to visit our website")

### WiFi QR Code
Encodes network name (SSID), password, and security type. When scanned, the phone automatically joins the network — no password typing needed. Perfect for cafes, offices, and events.

**Format**: `WIFI:S:NetworkName;T:WPA;P:password;;`

### vCard / Contact QR Code
Encodes a full contact card (name, phone, email, address). When scanned, the phone offers to save the contact.

**Best for**: Business cards, conference badges, networking events.

### Text QR Code
Encodes plain text. When scanned, displays the text directly in the scanner app.

**Best for**: Quick notes, short messages, or display text that doesn't need to be a link.

### Email QR Code
Opens the mail app with a pre-filled email address (and optionally subject and body).

### SMS QR Code
Opens the messages app with a pre-filled number and message.

## Best Practices for QR Code Design

### Minimum Size
- Print: At least 2cm × 2cm (roughly 0.8 inches)
- Screen: At least 100 × 100 pixels for comfortable scanning
- Outdoor/distance: Scale up proportionally with viewing distance

### Contrast
Black QR code on white background is the most reliably scanned. If you customize colors:
- Dark modules on light background (not reverse)
- Minimum contrast ratio of 4:1
- Avoid red on black or green on black

### Error Correction Level
QR codes have four error correction levels:
- **L (Low)**: 7% damage recovery, smallest/densest code
- **M (Medium)**: 15% recovery
- **Q (Quartile)**: 25% recovery
- **H (High)**: 30% recovery, largest code

Use **H level** when:
- Adding a logo (the logo covers part of the code)
- Printing on surfaces that may get scratched or worn
- Industrial or outdoor use

Use **L level** when:
- Maximum scanning speed matters
- The QR code will be displayed on clean screens

### Testing Before Deployment
Always test your QR code before printing or publishing:
1. Test on iOS (native Camera app)
2. Test on Android (Google Lens or Camera app)
3. Test in poor lighting conditions
4. Test from the maximum expected scanning distance

## Static vs. Dynamic QR Codes

### Static QR Codes
The content is encoded directly in the QR code. **Changing the destination requires generating a new code.**

- Free with most tools
- Content is permanent
- No tracking/analytics
- Works offline once generated

### Dynamic QR Codes
The QR code points to a redirect URL that you control. You can change the destination anytime without reprinting.

- Usually requires a paid service or subscription
- Enables analytics (scan counts, location, device type)
- Ideal for print materials with long shelf lives

For most personal and small business uses, static QR codes are sufficient and completely free.
